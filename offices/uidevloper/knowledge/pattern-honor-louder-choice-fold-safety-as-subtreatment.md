# Honor the CIO's louder choice — fold safety in as a sub-treatment, don't re-argue

During the DTC / check-engine design (2026-06-05), I recommended a **hybrid**
attention model (glanceable ribbon + takeover only for 🔴 STOP). The CIO chose the
**louder** option instead — a **full-screen takeover on every new code**. I honored it
immediately, then **baked the proportionality I'd been protecting INTO the takeover**:
the takeover is severity-styled (🔴 red "pull over" / 🟡 amber "drive gently" / 🟢 green
"safe to clear"), it fires only on a *new* code (ribbon thereafter), and STOP gets
Acknowledge-only while MINOR gets a calm info glyph. The honest-instrument / alarm-fatigue
goal survived — it just lives inside his choice instead of competing with it.

**Why:** the CIO owns the product decision; my recommendation is input, not a veto. But
the *reasons* behind a recommendation (here: don't train the driver to dismiss alarms;
a turbo misfire ≠ a gas cap) are usually still valid even when the top-level choice goes
the other way. Re-arguing the choice wastes his time and reads as not listening. Folding
the concern in as a sub-treatment respects the decision AND keeps the design honest.

**How to apply:**
- When a CIO pick overrides your recommendation, **say "got it" and move on** — don't
  relitigate. (Pairs with [[feedback-brainstorming-stall-nudge-pattern]] — keep momentum.)
- Then ask: *what was I actually protecting?* Find the sub-level where that concern can
  still live (color/severity styling, frequency rules, dismiss behavior, confirm gates)
  and design it in there.
- Surface it as "here's how I kept X working inside your choice," not "are you sure about
  your choice?" — show, get a quick confirm, proceed. (The CIO approved the severity-styled
  takeover instantly.)
- Non-negotiable SME safety semantics (Spool's tiers, clear-gate) are NOT yours to trade
  away regardless — they ride inside whatever surface the CIO picks. See
  [[pattern-ui-as-ssot-consumer]].
